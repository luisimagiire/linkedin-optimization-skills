## 1. Overview & How to Use This Guide

### Purpose

This document is a complete, self-contained roadmap for consultants to guide clients through optimizing their LinkedIn profile from scratch. It covers every section of a LinkedIn profile, in order, with discovery questions, templates, examples, and the reasoning behind each recommendation.

### Who Should Use This

This guide is designed for consultants with varying levels of LinkedIn expertise. Whether you are a seasoned social media specialist or a general business consultant with no prior LinkedIn optimization experience, this document provides everything you need to run a productive engagement from start to finish.

### Your Role as Consultant

You are **guiding the client to make changes themselves**. You do not edit the client's profile directly. Your job is to:

1. Run the discovery session to gather all necessary information.
2. For each profile section, explain what needs to be done and why.
3. Provide the client with templates, formulas, and examples.
4. Help the client draft and refine their content through iteration.
5. Review their work at each step to ensure quality.

### How the Flow Works

The process follows a **single linear sequence** that every client goes through:

1. **Phase 1: Discovery** -- One thorough upfront session to uncover the client's goal, background, target audience, and assets. No profile changes happen here.

**Pre-Step 0 (if needed):** If the client doesn't have a LinkedIn profile yet, guide them to linkedin.com to create one using their professional email. They'll need: full name, email, current job title, and company name to get started. This takes 5 minutes. Do this before Step 1.

2. **Steps 1-13: Profile Optimization** -- Each step covers one LinkedIn profile section in order. The consultant guides the client through each one sequentially.
3. **Final Quality Review** -- A lightweight review to confirm everything is in place.

### Adapting to the Client's Goal

While every client follows the same sequence, the *content* within each step adapts based on the client's primary goal. There are three core objectives:

- **Job Seeking** -- The client wants recruiters and hiring managers to find them and land interviews.
- **Lead Generation / Business Development** -- The client is a founder, consultant, or coach looking to attract clients.
- **Thought Leadership / Personal Branding** -- The client wants to build authority and a following in their niche.

During discovery, you will identify which goal applies. Throughout each step, this guide notes how to tailor the approach for each objective. When you see a "Goal Tailoring" callout, apply the guidance that matches your client's goal.

## 2. Phase 1: Discovery

### Why Discovery Matters

Discovery is the foundation of the entire engagement. Every decision you make during profile optimization -- from the headline wording to which experiences to highlight -- depends on the information gathered here. Skipping or rushing discovery leads to a generic profile that doesn't serve the client's specific objective.

This phase should be completed in a single session (typically 45-60 minutes). The consultant asks all questions before any profile changes begin.

### How to Conduct the Session

- Use a video call or in-person meeting so you can read the client's reactions and ask follow-ups.
- Take detailed notes or record the session (with permission).
- Where the client is unsure, note it and move on -- you can revisit specific items during the relevant execution step.
- If the client doesn't know their goal yet, help them decide using the questions below before proceeding.

---

### 2.1 Goal Identification

**Why:** The client's primary goal determines the entire strategy. A job seeker needs keyword density for recruiter searches. A lead generator needs a clear value proposition and calls to action. A thought leader needs a memorable positioning statement. Everything flows from this.

**Questions to ask:**

1. "What is the main reason you want to optimize your LinkedIn profile right now?"
2. "If your LinkedIn profile were working perfectly, what would be happening?" (Listen for specific outcomes like "recruiters reaching out," "clients finding me," "people recognizing my expertise")
3. "Are you currently looking for a new job, trying to attract clients, or building your personal brand?"
4. "Is there a secondary goal as well?" (Note it, but optimize for the primary one)

**How to classify the response:**

| What the client says | Primary Goal |
|---|---|
| "I want to get hired" / "I need a job" / "recruiters should find me" | **Job Seeking** |
| "I want more clients" / "I want leads" / "I want to grow my business" | **Lead Generation** |
| "I want to be known for X" / "I want to build authority" / "I want followers" | **Thought Leadership** |
| "I'm not sure" | Dig deeper with questions 1-2. If still unclear, ask: "If you could only have one result from LinkedIn in the next 90 days, what would it be?" |

**If the client is torn between goals:** Optimize for the one that has the most urgency or revenue impact. A profile can support a secondary goal, but trying to optimize for everything at once dilutes the result.

---

### 2.2 Target Audience / Ideal Client / Target Companies

**Why:** The profile must speak to a specific audience. Job seekers need to know which recruiters and hiring managers they want to attract. Lead generators need to know who their ideal client is. Thought leaders need to know who they're building authority with. Without a clear audience, the profile becomes generic.

**Questions to ask:**

1. "Who do you want to find and engage with your profile? Be as specific as possible."
2. "What type of companies or organizations are you targeting?" (For job seekers: specific companies, industries, or company sizes. For lead gen: types of businesses or client profiles.)
3. "What level of seniority is your target audience?" (e.g., C-suite, mid-level managers, entry-level, founders, VCs)
4. "What industry or industries does your target audience work in?"
5. "What is a 'Monday morning problem' your ideal client/target has?" (This is the problem that keeps them up at night. Picture your ideal client on a Monday morning -- what problem is sitting in their inbox that they need help with? This becomes the core of the positioning.)

**For Job Seekers specifically:**
6. "What specific job titles are you targeting?"
7. "Do you have a list of target companies?" (If yes, ask for it. These inform keyword research and headline crafting.)
8. "Are there specific recruiters you'd want to notice you?"

**For Lead Generation specifically:**
6. "Describe your ideal client in detail. What size company? What revenue? What stage of growth?"
7. "What is the typical budget or deal size?"
8. "What problem do you solve for them that they would pay for?"

**For Thought Leadership specifically:**
6. "What community or professional group do you want to be seen as an authority in?"
7. "What is the one word or concept you want people to associate with you?" (e.g., "systems" for someone who teaches productivity systems, "habits" for James Clear-style positioning)

---

### 2.3 Professional Background & Current Role

**Why:** The profile must tell a coherent story. You need to understand the client's full professional arc to decide which experiences to highlight, which to de-emphasize, and how to frame their narrative. The most effective profiles are focused, not exhaustive.

**Questions to ask:**

1. "What is your current role, company, and how long have you been there?"
2. "Walk me through your career path briefly -- what roles have you held and where?"
3. "What are the 2-3 roles or experiences from your background that are most relevant to your current goal?"
4. "Are there any roles or experiences you'd prefer not to highlight?" (Sometimes clients have experience that doesn't serve their current goal or that they don't want visible.)
5. "What are your top 3-5 professional achievements?" (Ask for specific, quantifiable results: revenue generated, team size managed, projects delivered, metrics improved.)
6. "Have you changed careers or industries? If so, what was the transition?" (This affects how you frame the narrative -- bridge/transferable skills become critical.)

---

### 2.4 Unique Value Proposition

**Why:** This is what makes the client stand out from everyone else with a similar job title. On LinkedIn, where thousands of people share the same role, the value proposition is the differentiator. It informs the headline, about section, and how experiences are framed.

**Questions to ask:**

1. "If a colleague or client were to recommend you, what would they say you're best at?"
2. "What do you do differently or better than most people in your field?"
3. "What is a result you've delivered that most people in your role haven't?"
4. "What would someone miss out on if they hired/worked with someone else instead of you?"
5. "Do you have any quantifiable results you can share?" (Revenue numbers, percentages, time saved, costs reduced, etc.)

**If the client struggles with this:** Ask for 3-5 specific stories of work they're proud of. Patterns in those stories usually reveal the value proposition. You can also ask: "What do people come to you for advice on?"

---

### 2.5 Keywords & Skills

**Why:** LinkedIn's search algorithm is keyword-driven. Recruiters search for specific terms. The right keywords, placed correctly, determine whether a profile appears in search results. Research shows relevant keywords should appear 8-10 times throughout a profile for optimal search ranking.

**Questions to ask:**

1. "What are the core skills or competencies you want to be known for?"
2. "What tools, technologies, methodologies, or frameworks do you work with?"
3. "What terms would someone search for to find someone with your expertise?"
4. "Are there industry-specific terms or certifications that are important in your field?"

**Action for the consultant (post-session):**

After discovery, the consultant should do independent keyword research:
1. Collect 5-10 job descriptions or target profiles relevant to the client's goal.
2. Paste them into a document and use a word cloud generator or ChatGPT to identify the top keywords by frequency.
3. Use the prompt: *"I am optimizing a LinkedIn profile for someone targeting [target role/field]. Analyze these [N] job descriptions and identify the top 10 keywords by frequency. Show the number of times each keyword appears."*
4. Select the top 3 most relevant keywords. Each should appear 8-10 times across the profile (headline: 1x each, space permitting -- at minimum include 1 of the top 3; about: 2-3x; experience: 3-5x; skills: 1x). Note: the low end of this range sums to 7; aim for the upper end by adding extra keyword mentions in experience descriptions where natural.


---

### 2.6 Industry & Niche

**Why:** Industry context shapes the tone, terminology, and framing of the entire profile. A software engineer's profile looks different from a management consultant's, even if both are optimizing for the same goal.

**Questions to ask:**

1. "What industry or industries do you work in?"
2. "Do you have a specific niche or specialization within that industry?"
3. "Are there industry norms or expectations for how professionals present themselves?" (e.g., finance tends to be more formal, creative industries more expressive)
4. "Who are the thought leaders or top voices in your space?" (Useful for reference research and understanding what good looks like in their niche.)

---

### 2.7 Career Narrative & Personal Story

**Why:** Facts tell, stories sell. The about section and headline benefit enormously from a compelling personal narrative. People connect with people, not resumes. The best LinkedIn profiles weave a story that makes the reader want to learn more.

**Questions to ask:**

1. "What drew you to your current field or career path?"
2. "Was there a defining moment, challenge, or experience that shaped your professional direction?"
3. "What are you most passionate about in your work?"
4. "What's something you've learned the hard way that you now use to help others?"
5. "Is there a personal angle or story you're comfortable sharing that makes you relatable?" (e.g., career pivot, overcoming adversity, unusual background)

**Note for the consultant:** Not every client will have a dramatic story, and that's fine. Even small authentic details -- why they chose this career, what they enjoy about Tuesday mornings -- make the profile feel human. Avoid manufacturing drama. Authenticity beats theatrics.

---

### 2.8 Current LinkedIn Status

**Why:** You need to know the starting point. A brand-new profile requires different guidance than one that's been active for years with 500+ connections.

**Questions to ask:**

1. "Do you currently have a LinkedIn profile?" (If no, they'll need to create one before Step 1.)
2. "When did you last update it?"
3. "Roughly how many connections do you have?"
4. "Is your profile currently set to 'Open to Work' or any similar status?"
5. "Do you have the Featured section visible on your profile?" (LinkedIn removed the Creator Mode toggle in March 2024 — creator tools like the Featured section are now available to all users by default.)
6. "On a scale of 1-10, how happy are you with your current profile?"

---

### 2.9 Privacy & Constraints

**Why:** Some clients have real constraints that affect what can appear on their profile. Ignoring these can create serious problems for the client.

**Questions to ask:**

1. "Is there anything about your current employment situation that limits what you can put on your profile?" (e.g., they're job searching but their current employer doesn't know)
2. "Are there any companies, roles, or dates you'd prefer to leave off?"
3. "Are you comfortable being publicly visible, or do you need a more conservative approach?"
4. "Do you have any non-compete or confidentiality agreements that affect what you can share?"
5. "Would you like to control who sees your activity and profile updates?"

**If the client is job searching discreetly:**
- Do NOT use "Open to Work" visible to all members. Use the recruiters-only option.
- Avoid obvious signals like "Seeking new opportunities" in the headline.
- The "I get excited about opportunities where I get to do X" phrasing in the About section is a subtle alternative.
- Turn off profile update notifications before making changes.

---

### 2.10 Contact Information & Availability

**Why:** The profile needs clear ways for people to reach the client. Different goals require different contact preferences.

**Questions to ask:**

1. "What's the best way for people to reach you?" (Email, phone, LinkedIn messaging, website)
2. "Do you have a personal website, portfolio, or landing page?"
3. "Do you have a Calendly or booking link for calls?"
4. "Are you comfortable sharing your email publicly on your profile?"

---

### 2.11 Assets Inventory

**Why:** Some profile elements require external assets. Knowing what the client already has prevents delays during execution.

**Questions to ask:**

1. "Do you have a professional headshot or high-quality photo you'd want to use?" (If not, they'll need to take one or generate one before Step 1.)
2. "Do you have any of the following: portfolio, case studies, published articles, GitHub, blog, YouTube channel, podcast?"
3. "Do you have any professional certifications or licenses?"
4. "Do you have any notable press mentions, speaking engagements, or awards?"
5. "Do you have existing recommendations or testimonials from colleagues, clients, or managers?" (If yes, they can be added. If no, you'll guide them on requesting them.)
6. "Do you have any volunteer experience relevant to your professional goals?"
7. "Are you a member of any professional organizations or industry associations?"

---

### 2.12 Discovery Session Wrap-Up

Before ending the discovery session:

1. Summarize what you heard back to the client: "Let me make sure I have this right -- your primary goal is [goal], you're targeting [audience], and your key differentiator is [value prop]. Is that accurate?"
2. Flag any gaps: "I noticed we didn't cover [area]. Can you tell me a bit about that?"
3. Confirm the client's goal classification (Job Seeking / Lead Generation / Thought Leadership) and note any secondary goals.
4. Explain what happens next: "Now that I have everything I need, our next sessions will go through each section of your LinkedIn profile one at a time. I'll guide you through what to write and how to set it up. You'll be making the changes yourself with my direction."
5. Assign any pre-work (e.g., "Please get a professional headshot taken before our next session" or "Send me links to 5 job postings you're interested in").

**Deliverable after discovery:** The consultant should have a completed discovery brief with all the information above, organized by section. This brief drives every decision in Steps 1-13.

## Step 1: Profile Photo

### Why This Matters

The profile photo is the single most visible element on LinkedIn. It appears everywhere -- in search results, comments, messages, post feeds, and the profile itself. LinkedIn data shows that profiles with a photo receive **14 times more views** than those without. The brain processes images faster than text, and people form conclusions within milliseconds. The photo sets the frame for every interaction that follows.

Think of the profile photo as packaging. If the packaging looks messy, blurry, or inappropriate, people skip it. If it looks professional and approachable, people lean in.

### What to Do

Guide the client to upload a professional, high-quality headshot as their profile photo.

### Requirements

| Must Have | Must Not Have |
|---|---|
| Face fills approximately 70% of the frame | Group photos, cropped party pictures |
| Clean, neutral background | Vacation selfies |
| Natural, even lighting (stand near a window) | Harsh shadows or dim lighting |
| Smiling or pleasant expression | Neutral/unfriendly expression |
| Recent (within last 2 years) | Outdated photos that no longer resemble them |
| Professional attire appropriate to their industry | Overly casual clothing (unless industry-appropriate) |

### How to Guide the Client

**If the client already has a good photo:**
- Review it together against the requirements above.
- Ask: "Does this photo represent how you want to be perceived professionally?"
- If it passes, move on.

**If the client needs a new photo:**
1. **Quick DIY method (free, same day):** Tell the client to grab their phone, stand near a window for natural light, ensure a clean background, and take a portrait shot. They should take 10-15 shots and pick the best one.
2. **AI-enhanced method (free, same day):** The client can use tools like PFPMaker.com or ChatGPT's image generation. Upload a casual photo and generate a professional-looking headshot. If using ChatGPT, prompt: *"Transform this photo into a professional LinkedIn headshot. Keep the face accurate."* For best results, upload multiple photos from different angles.
3. **Professional photographer (paid, scheduled):** If budget allows and quality matters, recommend a 15-minute headshot session. This is ideal for executives, founders, and consultants.

### Goal Tailoring

- **Job Seeking:** The photo should convey competence and reliability. Conservative professional attire is safest. Smile, but keep it polished.
- **Lead Generation:** The photo should convey confidence and approachability. Slightly more personality is welcome -- a warmer smile, slightly less formal attire if it fits the brand. Think "trusted advisor."
- **Thought Leadership:** The photo should convey authority while remaining human. This is where personality can come through more strongly. A slight creative element (interesting background, expressive look) is acceptable if it fits the personal brand.

### Common Mistakes

- **Using a selfie or casual snap:** Signals low effort. Even if the person is competent, the photo suggests otherwise.
- **Using a logo or graphic instead of a face:** LinkedIn is a people platform. People connect with faces, not logos.
- **Cropping someone out of a group photo:** Always looks awkward and suggests the client didn't invest in their profile.
- **Over-filtering or heavy editing:** If the person looks noticeably different in person, it creates a trust gap.

## Step 2: Cover Photo / Banner

### Why This Matters

The banner (also called the cover photo) is the large rectangular image behind the profile photo. It is the single largest piece of visual real estate on a LinkedIn profile. Most people leave it blank or use the default image -- which is a wasted opportunity.

The banner serves as a billboard. Within seconds of landing on a profile, the viewer has seen the photo and the banner together. A strategic banner communicates what the person does, who they help, and what to do next -- before the visitor reads a single word of text.

### What to Do

Guide the client to create and upload a custom banner that reinforces their positioning and includes a clear message.

### Banner Content Strategy

The banner should communicate one or more of the following:

1. **What the person does** (role or specialty)
2. **Who they help** (target audience)
3. **Their key value proposition or tagline**
4. **A call to action** (e.g., "Book a call," "Subscribe," "Download the guide")
5. **Contact information** (website, email)

Not all five are required. The most effective banners include 2-3 of these elements.

### Templates & Layout

**Use Canva (free):** Search for "LinkedIn banner" or "LinkedIn background photo" templates. Customize the text and colors to match the client's brand.

**Layout formula:**
- Left third: Leave relatively clear (the profile photo overlaps this area).
- Center and right two-thirds: Place text and graphics here.

**Template options by goal:**

- **Job Seeking banner:**
  ```
  [Name]
  [Target Role] | [2-3 Key Skills]
  [Contact: email@address.com]
  ```
  Clean, minimal. Communicates target role and key skills at a glance. Helps recruiters immediately see relevance.

- **Lead Generation banner:**
  ```
  [Headline / Value Proposition]
  "I help [audience] achieve [result]"
  [CTA: Book a free call | website link]
  ```
  Includes a call to action. Drives visitors toward the next step.

- **Thought Leadership banner:**
  ```
  [One word or phrase you own]
  [Tagline or memorable quote]
  [Newsletter / Community / Podcast link]
  ```
  Builds the personal brand. The banner should feel designed, not just informational. Consider using a professional design or brand colors.

### Examples

**Good:**
- A clean gradient background with the text "Helping SaaS founders scale from $1M to $10M" and a link to a free strategy call.
- A photo of the client speaking at a conference, with their name and "Data Analytics Leader" overlaid.
- A simple, well-designed banner with the client's tagline, website, and email.

**Bad:**
- The default LinkedIn blue background (signals zero effort).
- A company-provided banner that says nothing about the individual.
- A random landscape photo with no text (wasted space).
- A cluttered banner with too much text, making it unreadable.

### Goal Tailoring

- **Job Seeking:** Include target role, top 2-3 skills, and contact info. This acts as a visual headline for recruiters scrolling through search results.
- **Lead Generation:** Include value proposition and a call to action (book a call, visit website, download resource). The banner is a conversion tool.
- **Thought Leadership:** Include the one word/phrase the client owns, a tagline, and a link to their newsletter or community. The banner is a brand asset.

## Step 3: Custom URL

### Why This Matters

Every LinkedIn profile has a URL. By default, it looks something like:

`linkedin.com/in/john-smith-3a7b9c2x`

That default URL is unprofessional, hard to remember, and harder to share. A custom URL is clean, memorable, and easy to include on a resume, email signature, or business card. Research shows that resumes including a LinkedIn link have a **71% better chance** of getting a response.

Additionally, the custom URL contributes to SEO. When someone Googles the client's name, the LinkedIn profile is more likely to appear near the top when the URL includes the name.

### What to Do

Guide the client to edit their LinkedIn URL to a clean, professional format.

### How to Edit

1. Go to the LinkedIn profile page.
2. On the right side, find the "Edit your custom URL" option (pencil icon next to the URL).
3. Type the desired custom URL.
4. Save.

### URL Format Guidelines

| Good Examples | Bad Examples |
|---|---|
| `linkedin.com/in/johnsmith` | `linkedin.com/in/john-smith-3a7b9c2x` (default) |
| `linkedin.com/in/janesmith` | `linkedin.com/in/jane-smith-123` |
| `linkedin.com/in/firstnamelastname` | `linkedin.com/in/jsmith2019` |
| `linkedin.com/in/firstname-m-lastname` | `linkedin.com/in/john_the_boss` |

**Rules:**
- Use real name (first name + last name).
- If the exact name is taken, add a middle initial or use a hyphenated format (e.g., `john-m-smith`).
- Avoid numbers, underscores, or unprofessional words.
- Keep it as short and clean as possible.

### Goal Tailoring

- **Job Seeking:** This URL will go on resumes and applications. It must look professional at a glance.
- **Lead Generation:** The URL may be shared in email signatures, proposals, and marketing materials. It should be easy to type and remember.
- **Thought Leadership:** Same as above. The URL is part of the personal brand. It should be consistent with the name used everywhere else online.

### Common Mistakes

- **Leaving the default URL:** The random alphanumeric string looks unprofessional and forgettable.
- **Using numbers or special characters:** `john_smith_2024` or `jsmith123` looks unprofessional.
- **Not matching the resume name:** If the resume says "John Michael Smith," the URL should be `john-michael-smith` not `jsmith99`.
- **Using a nickname or informal name:** The URL should match the professional name used across all platforms.
- **For clients with common names:** If `johnsmith` is taken, try `john-m-smith`, `johnmsmith`, or `johnsmith1` as a last resort. Never use arbitrary numbers.

## Step 4: Headline

### Why This Matters

The headline is the line of text that appears directly below the profile photo. It is one of the most heavily weighted factors in LinkedIn's search algorithm. According to research, **46% of LinkedIn users consider the headline the most important section of a profile**, yet only **20% of users ever update it** from the default (which is just their current job title).

The headline appears everywhere -- in search results, comments, connection requests, messages, and post bylines. It is often the only piece of text a person reads before deciding whether to click into the profile or keep scrolling. A strong headline can be the difference between being clicked on or ignored.

### What to Do

Guide the client to write a compelling, keyword-rich headline using the two-part formula below.

### The Two-Part Headline Formula

**Part 1: Keyword-filled overview** -- What you do, using the terms your target audience searches for.

**Part 2: Value illustration with results** -- A quantifiable outcome or unique benefit you deliver.

**Structure:**
```
[Role / Specialty] | [Key Skills or Certifications] | [Value Proposition with Metrics]
```

### Templates by Goal

**For Job Seekers:**
```
[Target Role] | [2-3 Key Skills/Certifications] | [Quantified Achievement]
```
Examples:
- `Product Marketing Manager | Digital Advertising, Analytics | Helped 3 startups 2X their return on ad spend`
- `Data Scientist | Python, PyTorch, Machine Learning | Reduced ML training costs by 40% at scale`
- `Full Stack Software Engineer | Python, React, AWS | Increased website performance by 60% and user engagement by 35%`

**For Lead Generation:**
```
[Role/Identity] | [Who You Help] | [Result You Deliver]
```
Examples:
- `Sales Strategy Consultant | I help B2B founders | Close 2X more deals in 90 days`
- `Executive Coach | Helping leaders scale teams | From chaos to calm in 12 weeks`
- `Marketing Agency Owner | E-commerce brands | 3X ROAS with organic content systems`

**For Thought Leadership:**
```
[One-Liner / Word You Own] | [Audience] | [Credibility Marker]
```
Examples:
- `Systems for scaling | Founder of CompanyX | 500K+ followers on LinkedIn`
- `The Stoicism Guy | Author of "Daily Stoic" | 3M+ books sold`
- `Building in public | SaaS founder | Building CompanyX to $10M ARR`

### Keyword Placement

The headline should include **at least one of the client's top 3 target keywords** from the discovery research. This is the single most important place for keywords because LinkedIn weights headline keywords heavily in search ranking.

### Goal Tailoring

- **Job Seeking:** The headline should make the target role immediately clear and include skills that match target job descriptions. Focus on being findable in recruiter searches. Reference the Monday Morning Problem identified in discovery -- position the headline as the solution.
- **Lead Generation:** The headline should communicate the transformation the client delivers. Focus on who they help and the result. Think of it as a one-sentence value proposition. Reference the Monday Morning Problem -- the headline should speak directly to that pain.
- **Thought Leadership:** The headline should be memorable and own a specific word or concept. Focus on building recognition. Include a credibility marker (followers, features, rankings) if available.

### Common Mistakes

- **Using only a job title:** "Software Engineer at ABC Corp" makes the client look like 99% of LinkedIn users. The headline should communicate value, not just a label.
- **Using abbreviations:** "Sr. PM" might not match "Senior Product Manager" in recruiter searches. Use full terms.
- **Saying "Seeking opportunities" or "Open to work":** Recruiters do not search for those terms. This wastes valuable keyword space and can signal desperation.
- **Using buzzwords:** "Rockstar," "Ninja," "Guru," "Passionate" -- these provide no searchable keywords and come across as unprofessional.
- **Leaving the default headline:** The most common mistake. LinkedIn defaults to "Job Title at Company." Every client should customize this.

### How to Guide the Client

1. Ask the client: "If you had to describe what you do and the value you bring in one sentence, what would it be?"
2. Draft 2-3 options together using the templates above.
3. Check that at least one target keyword is present.
4. Check that a quantifiable result or benefit is included.
5. Review the character count (220 character limit) and trim if needed.
6. Ask: "If a stranger read only this line, would they know what you do and why they should care?"
7. Finalize and have the client update it in their profile intro section.

## Step 5: About / Summary Section

### Why This Matters

The About section (sometimes called the Summary) is the client's story in their own words. Research shows that **84% of people read at least the first sentence** of a LinkedIn About section, and **32% read the entire thing**. Perhaps most importantly, **65% of users -- including recruiters and hiring managers -- have reached out to someone they didn't know** because of something they read in that person's profile.

Profiles with a summary receive **up to 3.9 times more profile views** than those without one.

The About section is where the client moves from being a list of credentials to being a person. It bridges the gap between "what I've done" and "who I am and why you should work with me."

### What to Do

Guide the client to write a compelling About section using the template structure below.

### Template Structure (4 Parts)

**Part 1: Strong Opening Paragraph (2-3 sentences)**
Introduce who the client is, what they do, and their biggest differentiator. Include target keywords and a quantifiable metric. This paragraph must hook the reader, because many won't read further.

**Part 2: Core Competencies / Key Skills (3-5 bullet points)**
A scannable list of the client's strongest skills and areas of expertise. Use keywords from the discovery research.

**Part 3: Professional Story or Key Achievements (1-2 paragraphs)**
Briefly tell the story of the client's career arc, highlighting the most relevant experiences and achievements. Use specific numbers and results.

**Part 4: Signal of Availability + Contact Information**
A line indicating openness to new opportunities (worded appropriately for the goal), followed by contact details.

### Detailed Template

```
I am a [Role] with [X] years of experience in [Industry/Field]. 
I specialize in [Key Area 1], [Key Area 2], and [Key Area 3], 
helping [Target Audience] achieve [Specific Result].

Core competencies include:
- [Skill/Competency 1 with keyword]
- [Skill/Competency 2 with keyword]
- [Skill/Competency 3 with keyword]
- [Skill/Competency 4 with keyword]
- [Skill/Competency 5 with keyword]

[Professional Story: 2-4 sentences about career journey, key achievements, 
and what drives the client. Include quantified results where possible.]

I get excited about opportunities where I get to [Activity related to goal].

Connect with me at:
Email: [email]
Website: [URL]
[Other contact methods as appropriate]
```

### Examples

**Job Seeking Example:**
> I am a Product Marketing Manager with 6 years of experience in B2B SaaS. I specialize in go-to-market strategy, competitive analysis, and customer segmentation, helping growth-stage startups launch products that generate predictable revenue.
>
> Core competencies include:
> - Go-to-market strategy and product launches
> - Competitive intelligence and market positioning
> - Data-driven campaign optimization
> - Cross-functional team leadership
> - Customer segmentation and persona development
>
> I started my career in sales, which gives me a unique ability to bridge the gap between product teams and revenue teams. Most recently at CompanyX, I led the launch of ProductY, which generated $2.3M in pipeline within the first quarter. Before that, I built the competitive intelligence function from scratch at CompanyZ, reducing sales cycle length by 22%.
>
> I get excited about opportunities where I get to build something from the ground up and see it directly impact revenue.
>
> Email: jane@example.com
> Portfolio: janedoe.com

**Lead Generation Example:**
> I help B2B founders build predictable sales pipelines without relying on cold outreach or paid ads.
>
> After 8 years in enterprise sales -- where I personally closed over $12M in deals -- I realized that the best leads don't come from cold calls. They come from building authority and trust at scale.
>
> That's why I developed the Authority Pipeline System: a framework that combines LinkedIn content strategy, strategic networking, and automated nurturing to bring high-quality leads to you.
>
> Here's what I bring to the table:
> - LinkedIn profile optimization for lead conversion
> - Content strategy that positions you as the go-to expert in your niche
> - Automated lead nurturing workflows using CRM + LinkedIn
> - Sales funnel design from first touch to closed deal
> - Executive coaching on thought leadership and personal branding
>
> My clients typically see a 3-5X increase in inbound leads within 90 days.
>
> Want to see if this would work for you? Book a free strategy call at calendly.com/example.
>
> Email: mark@example.com
> Website: example.com

**Thought Leadership Example:**
> Systems thinker. Builder of calm, scalable businesses.
>
> I've spent the last 15 years building and growing online businesses. Along the way, I've learned that the difference between founders who burn out and founders who thrive isn't talent or luck -- it's systems.
>
> I write and teach about:
> - Deep work systems for founders
> - Content creation frameworks that scale
> - Revenue systems that don't depend on you being online 24/7
> - Team building and operational playbooks
> - Removing yourself from day-to-day operations
>
> My content reaches over 500,000 people monthly across LinkedIn and my newsletter. I've been ranked among the fastest-growing creators on the platform.
>
> If you're a founder looking to build a profitable personal brand with proven systems, join 20,000+ readers at my newsletter (link below).
>
> Email: alex@example.com
> Newsletter: example.com/newsletter

### Key Guidelines

1. **Write in first person.** Research shows 80% of LinkedIn users prefer profiles written as "I am..." rather than "Jane is..." First person is more conversational and authentic.

2. **Optimal length: 880-1,120 characters.** Long enough to be substantive, short enough to be readable. The About section can hold up to 2,600 characters, but the best profiles don't need all that space.

3. **Include 2-3 instances of each target keyword.** The About section is the second most important keyword location after the headline. Distribute keywords naturally -- don't stuff them.

4. **Use specific numbers.** "Increased revenue by 40%" is dramatically more compelling than "Helped grow revenue." "Managed a team of 12" beats "Led a large team." Quantifiable claims build instant credibility.

5. **Be conversational, not corporate.** The About section is the client's story, not a legal contract. It should read like how they'd introduce themselves at a professional dinner.

6. **Avoid buzzwords and jargon without context.** "Results-driven synergistic leader" means nothing. "I led a cross-functional team that reduced customer churn by 18%" means everything.

### Goal Tailoring

- **Job Seeking:**
  - Focus on skills, achievements, and relevance to the target role.
  - Use the "I get excited about opportunities where I get to do X" phrasing to subtly signal availability without tipping off a current employer.
  - Emphasize transferable skills if the client is changing industries.
  - Don't say "Looking for a job" -- instead, describe the kind of work they want to do.

- **Lead Generation:**
  - Lead with the client's value proposition and who they help.
  - Include a clear call to action (book a call, visit website, download resource).
  - Use social proof (client results, years of experience, revenue generated).
  - Position the client as a trusted advisor, not a salesperson.

- **Thought Leadership:**
  - Lead with the concept or word the client wants to own.
  - Tell their story with a focus on lessons learned and frameworks developed.
  - Include credibility markers (followers, features, rankings, publications).
  - Build toward a community or content asset (newsletter, podcast, community).

### Common Mistakes

- **Leaving it blank:** The most common mistake. A blank About section means 65% of potential connections have nothing to act on.
- **Writing in third person:** Feels stiff and impersonal. Switch to first person.
- **Listing responsibilities instead of achievements:** "Responsible for managing a team" vs. "Built and led a 12-person team that delivered $5M in annual revenue."
- **Being too generic:** "I am a passionate, motivated professional seeking new challenges." This could describe anyone. It describes no one.
- **Including too much:** The About section is a high-level overview. Save the deep details for the Experience section.

## Step 6: Featured Section

### Why This Matters

The Featured section is a curated showcase that sits prominently on the profile, directly below the About section. Items placed here cannot be missed by visitors -- they appear as large clickable cards with thumbnails. This section acts as a showcase window, allowing the client to highlight their best work, strongest content, or most important links front and center.

Unlike other sections that passively list information, the Featured section actively drives the visitor toward a specific action: reading a case study, visiting a portfolio, signing up for a newsletter, or viewing a top-performing post.

### What to Do

Guide the client to add 2-3 items to their Featured section that best support their goal.

### How to Set Up

The Featured section is now available to all LinkedIn users by default (LinkedIn removed the Creator Mode toggle in March 2024 — creator tools are now built into every profile). No special setup is needed to access it.

To add items to the Featured section:

1. Go to the profile page.
2. Find the Featured section (below About).
3. Click "Add featured" or the "+" icon.
4. Choose from: Posts, Articles, Links, or Media.

### What to Feature by Goal

**Job Seeking (2-3 items):**
1. A portfolio link or GitHub (if applicable) -- recruiters frequently look for proof of work.
2. A top-performing LinkedIn post or article that demonstrates expertise.
3. A link to a project, case study, or published work.

**Lead Generation (2-3 items):**
1. A lead magnet landing page (e.g., "Download my free guide") -- this converts profile visitors into email subscribers.
2. A case study or client testimonial post -- social proof that drives inquiries.
3. A booking link (e.g., Calendly) -- makes it frictionless to schedule a call.

**Thought Leadership (2-3 items):**
1. A newsletter signup page -- converts followers into subscribers.
2. The client's highest-performing LinkedIn post -- demonstrates reach and impact.
3. A link to their primary content asset (podcast, YouTube, blog, community).

### Guidelines

- **Less is more.** 2-3 items maximum. More than that dilutes focus and overwhelms the visitor.
- **Order matters.** Place the most important item first -- it gets the most visibility.
- **Use strong thumbnails.** If featuring a link, LinkedIn pulls the featured image from the URL. Ensure the destination page has a compelling image. If featuring a post, the post's image becomes the thumbnail.
- **Keep it current.** Review and update the Featured section quarterly. Remove outdated items and replace with fresh ones.

### Common Mistakes

- **Leaving it empty:** A blank Featured section is a missed conversion opportunity.
- **Featuring too many items:** 5+ featured items become a scroll-fest. 2-3 is the sweet spot.
- **Featuring irrelevant content:** A post about a vacation or personal milestone doesn't serve the profile's objective. Feature work, not life updates.
- **Not checking how thumbnails appear:** Broken or missing thumbnails look unprofessional.

## Step 7: Experience Section

### Why This Matters

The Experience section is where the client proves they can deliver on the promises made in their headline and about section. It is the evidence section. Recruiters, clients, and collaborators read this to assess whether the client has the track record they claim.

This section is also critical for keyword optimization. LinkedIn's search algorithm heavily weights the experience section -- target keywords should appear **3-5 times per keyword** across the client's listed experiences.

### What to Do

Guide the client to list their work experience strategically, emphasizing relevance and impact over completeness.

### Key Principle: Relevance Over Completeness

The most effective profiles do **not** list every job the client has ever held. They list only the experiences that are relevant to the client's current goal.

- A focused profile that highlights 2-4 relevant, well-described positions is stronger than a sprawling list of 10+ roles with minimal detail.
- Remove or minimize experiences that don't serve the current objective. However, be mindful of creating visible date gaps. If removing an experience would leave a noticeable gap, keep it listed with minimal detail (title, company, dates only) rather than omitting it entirely.
- Experience does not only have to be a paid job. Mentorships, volunteer work, internships, freelance projects, and significant coursework can all count if they are relevant.

### How to Structure Each Entry

For each position, the client should include:

1. **Job Title** -- Use a title that aligns with the target role (not necessarily the official internal title if it's obscure).
2. **Company Name**
3. **Employment Type** (Full-time, Part-time, Contract, Freelance, Internship)
4. **Dates** (Month/Year start - Month/Year end, or "Present")
5. **Location**
6. **Description** -- This is where the real work happens. Use bullet points.

### Bullet Point Formula

For each bullet point, use this structure:

```
[What I did] + [For whom / In what context] + [Measurable outcome or result]
```

Every bullet should answer at least two of these three questions:
- **What** did I do?
- **For whom** did I do it?
- **What was the result?** (quantified whenever possible)

### Examples

**Good bullet points:**
- "Led a cross-functional team of 12 engineers to launch ProductX, generating $2.3M in pipeline within the first quarter."
- "Built the company's first competitive intelligence function, reducing average sales cycle length by 22% across a 50-person sales team."
- "Designed and executed a content marketing strategy that grew organic traffic from 10K to 120K monthly visitors in 8 months."
- "Managed a $5M annual paid media budget, achieving a consistent 4.2X return on ad spend across Google and Meta campaigns."

**Bad bullet points:**
- "Responsible for managing the marketing team." (No outcome, no specificity.)
- "Worked on various projects." (Says nothing.)
- "Handled social media." (For whom? What result?)
- "Team player with strong communication skills." (This is not an achievement, it's a personality trait.)

### Keyword Placement in Experience

Each listed experience should naturally incorporate target keywords in the bullet points. For each of the client's top 3 keywords:

- Aim for 3-5 total appearances across all experience entries.
- Distribute them naturally -- do not force keywords where they don't fit.
- Keywords can appear in: job titles, bullet point descriptions, skills listed per role, and media/links attached to each role.

### How Many Positions to List

| Scenario | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Client has 2-4 highly relevant positions | List all with full bullet points (3-5 bullets each) |
| Client has 5+ positions, some not relevant | List 3-4 most relevant in detail; briefly list others or omit them |
| Client is early-career with limited experience | Include internships, projects, volunteer work, and relevant coursework |
| Client is changing careers | Emphasize transferable/bridge skills in each past role's description |
| Client has gaps in employment | Focus on what they did during gaps (freelance, learning, projects) and frame positively |

### How to Guide the Client

1. Review the client's career history from discovery.
2. Together, select the 2-4 most relevant positions to highlight.
3. For each position, draft 3-5 bullet points using the formula above.
4. Push the client for specific numbers: "How much revenue? How big was the team? What was the percentage improvement? How many users?"
5. Check that target keywords appear naturally across the descriptions.
6. Have the client enter each position into their LinkedIn profile.

### Goal Tailoring

- **Job Seeking:**
  - Bullet points should demonstrate capability and results that map directly to the target role's requirements.
  - Use keywords from the target job descriptions.
  - Frame every achievement in terms of business impact.
  - If changing careers, emphasize transferable skills and reframe past achievements in the language of the target field.

- **Lead Generation:**
  - Bullet points should demonstrate client results and business outcomes.
  - Frame achievements in terms of revenue, growth, and client success.
  - Highlight experience that builds credibility with the target client type.
  - Quantify everything: "Generated $X in revenue," "Worked with X clients," "Delivered X% improvement."

- **Thought Leadership:**
  - Bullet points should demonstrate depth of expertise and thought leadership milestones.
  - Highlight publications, speaking engagements, and content creation milestones.
  - Include community-building and mentoring experiences.
  - Show a trajectory of increasing influence and impact.

### Common Mistakes

- **Listing every job ever held:** A profile with 12+ positions is overwhelming and dilutes focus. Include only what serves the current goal.
- **Vague bullet points:** "Responsible for marketing" tells the reader nothing. Every bullet should include an outcome.
- **Copying job descriptions verbatim:** LinkedIn profiles should highlight achievements, not list duties. A job description says "manage a team." A profile should say "Built and led a 12-person team that delivered $5M in annual revenue."
- **Listing responsibilities instead of outcomes:** "Managed the sales pipeline" vs. "Rebuilt the sales pipeline, reducing average deal close time from 45 to 28 days."
- **No quantifiable results:** If a bullet point contains no numbers, the client is underselling themselves. Push for metrics: how much, how many, what percentage, how long.
- **Inconsistent date formatting:** Use Month/Year consistently across all entries. Gaps in dates raise questions.
- **Omitting relevant keywords:** The experience section is the primary keyword location (3-5x per keyword). If keywords aren't present here, search ranking suffers significantly.

## Step 8a: Education Section

### Why This Matters

The Education section provides context and credibility. While not as critical as Experience for most professionals, it still matters -- especially for recent graduates, career changers, and roles where educational background is a screening criterion.

### What to Do

Guide the client to add their educational background, emphasizing relevance.

### How to Structure Each Entry

For each institution:

1. **School Name**
2. **Degree** (e.g., Bachelor of Science, Master of Business Administration)
3. **Field of Study** (e.g., Computer Science, Marketing, Economics)
4. **Dates Attended** (Start Year - End Year)
5. **Grade/GPA** (Optional -- include only if strong and relevant)
6. **Activities and Societies** (Optional -- include if relevant to the target goal)
7. **Description** (Optional -- use only if there's something relevant to add, like a thesis topic, research, or notable achievement)

### Guidelines

- **List in reverse chronological order** (most recent first).
- **Keep it concise.** Unlike the Experience section, Education entries don't need lengthy descriptions.
- **Relevance matters here too.** If the client has multiple degrees, prioritize the ones most relevant to their current goal.
- **For recent graduates (less than 3 years of experience):** Education can be listed above Experience on the profile. Include relevant coursework, honors, capstone projects, and extracurricular leadership.
- **For experienced professionals (3+ years):** Education is less prominent. Include it but keep it brief. The focus should be on Experience.
- **If the client attended a prestigious institution:** Name-drop it. It builds credibility.
- **If the client doesn't have a degree:** This section is optional. Focus on certifications, projects, and experience instead. Do not fabricate or exaggerate education.

### Examples

**Good -- Recent graduate:**
> **University of Michigan** | Bachelor of Science in Computer Science | 2019-2023
> GPA: 3.8/4.0 | Relevant Coursework: Data Structures, Machine Learning, Distributed Systems
> Activities: President of Women in Tech (2022-2023), Hackathon Organizer

**Good -- Experienced professional:**
> **Stanford University** | Master of Business Administration | 2015-2017
> Focus: Strategy & Operations

**Weak:**
> Stanford | MBA | 2017 (Too sparse -- no context about focus or achievements)

### Goal Tailoring

- **Job Seeking:**
  - If recent graduate: include GPA (if 3.5+), relevant coursework, academic projects, and extracurricular leadership.
  - If experienced: keep it brief. Degree, field, and institution are sufficient.
  - If the degree is from a prestigious institution, let it speak for itself.

- **Lead Generation:**
  - Keep it brief unless the degree is directly relevant to the services offered.
  - An MBA or specialized degree can build credibility with clients.

- **Thought Leadership:**
  - If the degree is in the client's area of thought leadership, include more detail (research focus, thesis, publications).
  - Academic credibility reinforces authority.

### Common Mistakes

- **Listing every single course or activity:** Keep it to highlights that are relevant.
- **Omitting education entirely:** Even if it's not from a top school, it fills out the profile and avoids gaps.
- **Including GPA if it's low:** Only include if it's a strength.
- **Not including relevant coursework for recent graduates:** For entry-level job seekers, relevant courses demonstrate knowledge when experience is limited.

## Step 8b: Volunteer Experience & Organizations

### Why This Matters

LinkedIn has a dedicated Volunteer Experience section separate from Work Experience. For early-career professionals, career changers, and non-traditional candidates, volunteer work is often the most relevant and compelling experience on their profile. It demonstrates initiative, character, and skills that may not be visible in paid roles. For experienced professionals, volunteer leadership shows community engagement and breadth of capability.

### What to Do

Guide the client to add relevant volunteer experience and professional organization memberships.

### When This Section Matters Most

| Client Type | Importance |
|---|---|
| Recent graduates with limited work experience | High -- volunteer work fills the experience gap |
| Career changers | High -- volunteer work in the target field demonstrates commitment |
| Experienced professionals | Low-Medium -- include if it demonstrates leadership or relevant skills |
| Non-profit / social impact professionals | High -- core to their professional identity |

### How to Structure Each Entry

1. **Role / Title** (e.g., "Volunteer Marketing Advisor," "Board Member")
2. **Organization Name**
3. **Cause** (LinkedIn provides a dropdown)
4. **Dates**
5. **Description** (1-3 sentences describing the contribution and impact)

### Examples

**Good:**
- "Pro Bono Marketing Consultant | Local Food Bank | 2022-Present | Developed and executed a social media strategy that increased monthly donations by 35%. Mentored a team of 3 junior volunteers on content creation."
- "Board Member | Women in Tech NYC | 2020-2023 | Led the mentorship program matching 50+ women with senior industry mentors. Organized 12 networking events with 200+ attendees."

**Weak:**
- "Volunteer at local charity." (Too vague -- what did they do? What impact?)

### Professional Organizations

If the client is a member of professional organizations (industry associations, alumni groups, chambers of commerce), these can also be listed. They signal that the client is engaged in their professional community.

### Goal Tailoring

- **Job Seeking:** Include volunteer work that demonstrates skills relevant to the target role. For career changers, volunteer work in the target field is especially valuable.
- **Lead Generation:** Include volunteer work that builds credibility with the target client type. Advisory board roles and pro bono consulting demonstrate generosity and expertise.
- **Thought Leadership:** Include leadership roles in professional organizations. Community involvement reinforces authority and commitment to the field.

### Common Mistakes

- **Omitting volunteer experience entirely:** Especially for early-career clients, this is wasted proof of capability.
- **Listing volunteer work without describing impact:** "Volunteered at XYZ" is not compelling. Describe the contribution and results.
- **Including irrelevant volunteer work:** A software engineer's volunteer dog-walking is less relevant than their volunteer coding for a non-profit.

## Step 9: Skills & Endorsements

### Why This Matters

The Skills section is one of the primary ways LinkedIn's algorithm matches profiles to jobs and recruiter searches. According to LinkedIn's own data, profiles with **5 or more skills receive 17 times more views** on average. Additionally, **45% of recruiters actively reference the skills section** when evaluating candidates.

However, there's a critical detail most people miss: LinkedIn's search algorithm only factors in a skill if it has **at least one endorsement**. A skill with zero endorsements is effectively invisible to the algorithm. Furthermore, endorsements from professionals who already have that same skill on their own profile carry more weight.

This section is not just a list -- it is a search visibility tool.

### What to Do

Guide the client to add their top skills and obtain endorsements for them.

### How to Select Skills

1. Start with the keyword research from discovery (Section 2.5).
2. Use ChatGPT or the word cloud analysis from the 5-10 target job descriptions to identify the top 10 most relevant skills.
3. From that list, select the **top 5** to prioritize for endorsements. These should be the skills most critical to the client's goal.
4. Add up to 50 skills total (LinkedIn's maximum), but focus endorsement efforts on the top 5.

**ChatGPT prompt for skill identification:**
```
I am optimizing a LinkedIn profile for someone targeting [target role/field].
I will share 5 job descriptions they are interested in.
Your task is to identify the top 10 most relevant skills they should include.
Sort by most relevant to least relevant.
Here are the 5 job descriptions: [paste descriptions]
```

### Adding Skills on LinkedIn

1. Go to the profile page.
2. Navigate to the Skills section.
3. Click "Add a new skill."
4. Search for and add skills one at a time.
5. LinkedIn allows pinning up to **3 skills** to the top -- pin the 3 most important ones.

### Getting Endorsements

For each of the top 5 skills, the client needs at least one endorsement (ideally 3-5). Here's how to get them:

1. **Identify people to ask:** Colleagues, former managers, clients, classmates, friends in the same industry.
2. **The give-first strategy:** Endorse others for their skills first. Many will reciprocate without being asked.
3. **Direct outreach:** Send a brief message: "Hey [Name], I'm updating my LinkedIn profile and would appreciate an endorsement on [Skill]. Happy to endorse you in return!"
4. **Prioritize endorsers who have the same skill on their profile** -- their endorsement carries more algorithmic weight.

### Goal Tailoring

- **Job Seeking:**
  - Skills should directly match the requirements in target job descriptions.
  - Prioritize hard skills and technical competencies (tools, technologies, methodologies).
  - Pin the 3 skills most frequently mentioned in target job postings.
  - The recruiter search tool has a specific filter for skills -- being listed here is how you show up.

- **Lead Generation:**
  - Skills should reflect the services the client offers and the problems they solve.
  - Mix hard skills (e.g., "Sales Strategy," "Content Marketing") with outcome-oriented skills (e.g., "Business Development," "Revenue Growth").
  - Emphasize skills that build trust with potential clients.

- **Thought Leadership:**
  - Skills should align with the topics the client creates content about.
  - Include both domain expertise skills and content/communication skills.
  - Prioritize skills that reinforce the "word they own" from their positioning.

### Common Mistakes

- **Adding skills without endorsements:** Unendorsed skills don't count in LinkedIn's search algorithm. Always follow up with endorsement requests.
- **Adding irrelevant skills:** "Microsoft Word" on a senior executive's profile is wasted space. Every skill should serve the goal.
- **Not pinning the top 3:** Pinned skills appear prominently on the profile. Use them for the most important ones.
- **Ignoring the Skills section entirely:** Many people skip it because it seems minor. It's actually one of the easiest wins for search visibility.

## Step 10: Recommendations

### Why This Matters

Recommendations are written testimonials from colleagues, managers, clients, or partners that appear on the client's profile. They are the most powerful form of social proof on LinkedIn. Think of them as five-star reviews displayed directly on a professional storefront.

Recommendations provide third-party validation that the client's claims are true. A profile that says "I'm a great leader" is a claim. A profile with a recommendation from a former direct report saying "The best manager I've ever had -- she built a culture where our team delivered 30% above target for 8 consecutive quarters" is proof.

### What to Do

Guide the client to request 2-3 recommendations from credible sources.

### Who to Ask

Select recommenders based on the client's goal:

| Goal | Best Recommenders |
|---|---|
| Job Seeking | Former managers, senior colleagues, cross-functional partners who can speak to impact |
| Lead Generation | Past or current clients who can speak to results delivered |
| Thought Leadership | Industry peers, mentees, community members who can speak to influence and expertise |

### How to Request a Recommendation

1. **Give first.** Write a recommendation for the person before asking for one in return. This dramatically increases the response rate.
2. **Be specific in the request.** Don't say "Can you write me a recommendation?" Instead, guide the recommender:
   - "Would you be willing to write a brief LinkedIn recommendation for me? If it helps, it would be great if you could mention [specific project we worked on together] and [the result we achieved]."
3. **Make it easy.** Offer to draft talking points or a rough outline they can edit. Most people want to help but don't know what to write.
4. **Follow up.** People are busy. A gentle reminder after 1-2 weeks is appropriate.

### What a Good Recommendation Looks Like

A strong recommendation includes:
- The relationship context ("I managed Jane for 2 years at CompanyX")
- A specific observation of skill or impact ("She single-handedly rebuilt our onboarding process, reducing ramp-up time by 40%")
- A personal touch ("Beyond her technical skills, Jane is the kind of person who makes everyone around her better")
- An endorsement ("I would hire Jane again in a heartbeat")

**Good example:**
> "I had the pleasure of working with Mark for 18 months at CompanyX. During that time, he helped us rebuild our entire sales pipeline from scratch. The new process increased our close rate from 12% to 28% and added $1.2M in annual revenue. Mark has a rare ability to combine strategic thinking with hands-on execution. He's also genuinely enjoyable to work with -- calm under pressure, direct in communication, and always focused on what matters most. I'd work with him again without hesitation."

**Weak example:**
> "Jane is a great colleague and hard worker. She is very professional and dedicated."

### How Many Recommendations

- **Minimum:** 2-3 strong recommendations.
- **Ideal:** 5+ recommendations from different roles and time periods, showing a pattern of consistent excellence.
- **Quality over quantity:** One detailed, specific recommendation is worth more than five generic ones.

### Common Mistakes

- **Not asking at all:** Most people don't ask because it feels awkward. Reframe it: people generally enjoy being asked -- it's a compliment.
- **Asking too many people at once:** Start with 3-5 requests. More than that becomes unmanageable.
- **Getting generic recommendations:** "Great person to work with" doesn't help. Guide recommenders toward specificity.
- **Only asking current colleagues:** A mix of past and present, managers and peers, creates a more well-rounded picture.

### Goal Tailoring

- **Job Seeking:**
  - **Who to prioritize:** Former managers who can speak to your impact and work quality, plus cross-functional partners who can vouch for collaboration skills.
  - **What the recommendation should emphasize:** Quantifiable results, leadership, problem-solving, and reliability. The recommender should explicitly mention the type of work you want to continue doing.
  - **How to guide the recommender:** "It would be especially helpful if you could mention [specific project] and [the measurable outcome]."

- **Lead Generation:**
  - **Who to prioritize:** Past and current clients who can speak to the results you delivered. Client recommendations are the most powerful social proof for lead generation.
  - **What the recommendation should emphasize:** Business outcomes (revenue, growth, time saved), the client experience of working with you, and your reliability. The recommendation should make a potential client think "I want those results too."
  - **How to guide the recommender:** "It would be great if you could mention [specific result we achieved together] and what the experience was like."

- **Thought Leadership:**
  - **Who to prioritize:** Industry peers, mentees, and community members who can speak to your expertise, generosity, and influence.
  - **What the recommendation should emphasize:** Depth of knowledge, willingness to share expertise, impact on others' careers or thinking, and thought leadership contributions.
  - **How to guide the recommender:** "If you could mention [how we worked together] and [what you took away from it], that would be ideal."

## Step 11: Projects, Certifications & Licenses

### Why This Matters

These sections provide tangible proof of skills and continuous learning. They fill out the profile, demonstrate initiative, and can be differentiators -- especially for job seekers competing against candidates with similar backgrounds.

### Projects Section

**What to include:**
- Personal projects relevant to the client's field.
- Open source contributions.
- Significant work projects (if not confidential).
- Capstone or thesis projects (for recent graduates).
- Side projects that demonstrate skills.

**For each project:**
1. Project name
2. Description (2-4 sentences: what it is, what technologies/tools were used, and the outcome)
3. Link to repository, live demo, or documentation
4. Dates
5. Collaborators (optional)

**Goal Tailoring:**
- **Job Seeking:** Projects are proof of ability. Include anything that demonstrates skills from target job descriptions. A portfolio of 2-3 well-documented projects is a significant advantage.
- **Lead Generation:** Include case studies or client projects (with permission). These serve as social proof of deliverable results.
- **Thought Leadership:** Include community projects, open source contributions, or content initiatives that demonstrate thought leadership in action.

**Examples:**

*Good project entry:*
> **Customer Churn Prediction Model** | Jan 2024 - Mar 2024
> Built an ML model using Python and scikit-learn to predict customer churn with 89% accuracy. Deployed via AWS SageMaker and integrated with the company's CRM. Result: Enabled the retention team to proactively reach out to at-risk customers, reducing churn by 15%.
> Link: github.com/username/churn-model

*Good project entry (lead gen context):*
> **Case Study: Scaling a SaaS Startup from $500K to $2M ARR** | 2023
> Developed and executed a full-funnel marketing strategy including LinkedIn content, email nurture sequences, and paid acquisition. Achieved a 3.2X return on ad spend and grew monthly qualified leads from 20 to 85.

### Certifications & Licenses Section

**What to include:**
- Professional certifications (AWS, PMP, Google Analytics, etc.)
- Industry licenses (CPA, Series 7, bar admission, etc.)
- Completed courses from recognized platforms (Coursera, Udacity, etc.)
- Online certifications that are relevant and credible

**For each certification:**
1. Certification name
2. Issuing organization
3. Date obtained (and expiration date if applicable)
4. Credential ID or link (if available)

**Examples:**

*Good certification entry:*
> **AWS Solutions Architect - Associate** | Amazon Web Services | Feb 2024 | Credential ID: ABC123

*Good certification entry (with link):*
> **Google Analytics 4 Certification** | Google | Jan 2024 | [Verify credential](https://example.com)

**Goal Tailoring:**
- **Job Seeking:** Prioritize industry-standard certifications that appear in target job descriptions. If a certification is listed as "required" or "preferred" in target JDs, it should be on the profile.
- **Lead Generation:** Include certifications that build client credibility. If potential clients would be reassured by a certification (e.g., CPA for financial consultants, PMP for project management consultants), include it.
- **Thought Leadership:** Include certifications that reinforce niche expertise. Academic or research credentials are especially valuable here.

**Guidelines:**
- Only include certifications that are relevant to the client's goal.
- Prioritize well-known, recognized certifications over obscure ones.
- Free certifications from reputable platforms are fine -- the key is relevance.
- If the client is between roles, earning a relevant certification is a productive use of time that also signals proactivity.

### Common Mistakes

- **Listing irrelevant projects or certifications:** A baking certification on a software engineer's profile is distracting.
- **Including expired certifications without noting the expiration:** Keep them current or remove them.
- **Not linking to project evidence:** A project description without a link to see the actual work is just a claim. Provide proof.
- **Omitting this section entirely:** It signals a lack of initiative. Even 1-2 items are better than none.

### Honors & Awards (Optional)

LinkedIn has a dedicated "Honors & Awards" section. For some clients this is relevant and worth including:

**When to include:**
- Industry awards, recognitions, or rankings (e.g., "Top 40 Under 40" lists)
- Academic honors (Dean's List, Summa Cum Laude, Valedictorian)
- Competition wins (hackathons, case competitions)
- Speaking engagements and keynote invitations
- Community or professional recognition

**When to skip:**
- Generic or unverified awards
- Awards from unknown organizations
- Anything that doesn't reinforce the client's professional positioning

**Guideline:** One strong, relevant award is better than none. Skip this section if none apply. Only add awards that strengthen credibility with the target audience.

## Step 12: Open to / Recruiter Preferences

### Why This Matters

LinkedIn has built-in features that signal availability to specific audiences. When configured correctly, these features make the profile appear in targeted recruiter searches and signal intent without broadcasting it to the entire world. Using the "Open to Work" feature makes a profile **40% more likely to receive recruiter messages** and **20% more likely to receive member messages**.

However, this feature must be used carefully -- not every client should be visibly "open to work."

### What to Do

Guide the client to configure their availability settings based on their goal and privacy constraints.

### For Job Seeking Clients

**Open to Work settings:**
1. Go to the profile page.
2. Click "Open to" (near the top of the profile).
3. Select "Finding a new job."
4. Fill in:
   - Job titles (list 2-5 target titles -- use keywords from discovery)
   - Job locations (remote, hybrid, specific cities)
   - Start date (immediately, or specific date)
   - Employment types (full-time, part-time, contract)
5. **Visibility setting -- choose carefully:**
   - **"All LinkedIn members"**: Adds an "Open to Work" frame around the profile photo. Visible to everyone including current employer. Use only if the client is openly job searching.
   - **"Recruiters only"**: Visible only to people using LinkedIn Recruiter (i.e., professional recruiters). Does NOT add the photo frame. Use this if the client is job searching discreetly.

### For Lead Generation Clients

The "Open to" feature is generally not applicable. Instead:
- Ensure the profile clearly communicates that the client is available for work (through the About section, Featured section, and banner).
- The "Open to providing services" option (if available) can be enabled to appear in searches for service providers.

### For Thought Leadership Clients

The "Open to" feature is generally not applicable. Instead:
- Enable "Make follow primary" in Settings (see Step 13 for setup) to prioritize audience building.
- The profile itself should communicate authority and availability for speaking, consulting, or collaboration through the About section and Featured section.

### Common Mistakes

- **Using "Open to Work" with all-members visibility when the current employer shouldn't know:** This is the most damaging mistake. Always confirm the client's privacy situation first (covered in discovery, Section 2.9).
- **Not using the feature at all when job seeking:** Leaving this off means missing out on a 40% boost in recruiter outreach.
- **Being too narrow in job titles:** Listing only one title limits search visibility. List 2-5 related titles.
- **Forgetting to update when hired:** The "Open to Work" status should be removed once the client has accepted a role.

## Step 13: Profile Settings & Privacy

### Why This Matters

Before and during the optimization process, certain LinkedIn settings need to be configured to ensure a smooth experience and protect the client's privacy. Making profile changes without adjusting these settings can create unwanted notifications to the client's network or expose activity the client wants to keep private.

### What to Do

Guide the client through the following settings.

### 1. Turn Off Profile Update Notifications

**Why:** By default, LinkedIn notifies the client's entire network every time they make a change to their profile. When the client is making multiple updates during optimization, this floods connections with notifications -- which is annoying and, for job seekers, can alert their current employer.

**How:**
1. Go to Settings & Privacy.
2. Navigate to Visibility.
3. Find "Visibility of your LinkedIn activity."
4. Find "Share profile updates with your network."
5. Turn this OFF before making any profile changes.

**When to turn it back on:** After all optimization is complete, the client can turn this back on so that future updates (new roles, certifications, etc.) are shared.

### 2. Set "Make Follow Primary" (Replaces former Creator Mode)

**Background:** LinkedIn removed the Creator Mode on/off toggle in March 2024. Creator tools (Featured section, analytics, newsletters) are now available to all users by default — no toggle needed. The one setting that still matters is whether "Follow" or "Connect" is the primary button on the profile.

**Why:** For Thought Leadership and Lead Generation clients, "Follow" is the better primary action. It allows unlimited audience growth (connections are capped at 30,000) and signals that the profile is a source of valuable content. Visitors can still connect via "More" → "Connect."

**How to enable "Make follow primary":**
1. Go to **Settings & Privacy** (click your photo, top right → Settings & Privacy).
2. Navigate to **Visibility** → **Visibility of your LinkedIn activity**.
3. Find **"Make follow primary"** and toggle it **ON**.
4. On mobile: Settings → Visibility → Followers → Turn on "Make follow primary."

**Who should use it:**
- **Thought Leadership clients:** Yes. "Follow" is the better primary action since the goal is building an audience.
- **Lead Generation clients:** Yes. Building a follower base supports long-term lead generation and content visibility.
- **Job Seeking clients:** Optional. "Connect" may be preferable to facilitate direct networking. The Featured section is available regardless of this setting — no toggle is needed to access it anymore.

**Note:** Under the "Who can follow you" setting, choose **"Everyone on LinkedIn"** for maximum reach. Switching away from this later will remove existing out-of-network followers.

### 3. Profile Visibility

**Why:** By default, LinkedIn profiles are visible to all members and appear in search engine results. Some clients may want to restrict this.

**Settings to check:**
1. **Public profile visibility:** Controls whether the profile appears in Google search results. Most clients should keep this ON for maximum visibility.
2. **Profile viewing options:** The client can choose whether their name appears when they view other profiles, or if they appear anonymous. For active networking, visible profile viewing is better.

### 4. Contact Information

**Why:** The contact info section (accessible from the profile intro area) controls what personal details are visible. It should be configured to match the client's goal.

**What to verify:**
- Email address is present and correct.
- Website/portfolio links are added.
- Phone number (if appropriate).
- Twitter/X or other social handles (if relevant).
- Birthday (optional -- some people include it, others don't. No strong recommendation either way).

### 5. Name Pronunciation

**Why:** LinkedIn allows recording a short audio clip of name pronunciation. This is a small but professional touch that signals attention to detail and helps people address the client correctly.

**Recommendation:** If the client's name is commonly mispronounced or they work in international contexts, add this feature.

## Final Quality Review

### Why This Matters

After completing all 13 steps, a quick review ensures nothing was missed and the profile works as a cohesive whole. This is not an exhaustive audit -- it is a sanity check to catch obvious gaps or inconsistencies.

### Review Checklist

Go through the following with the client:

**Visual First Impression:**
- [ ] Profile photo is professional, recent, and fills ~70% of the frame
- [ ] Banner is custom (not default) and reinforces the client's positioning
- [ ] Custom URL is clean and includes the client's name

**Content Quality:**
- [ ] Headline uses the two-part formula (keyword overview + value/results)
- [ ] Headline contains at least one target keyword
- [ ] About section is written in first person
- [ ] About section includes 2-3 instances of each target keyword
- [ ] About section contains quantifiable achievements
- [ ] About section has a call to action or signal of availability (appropriate to goal)

**Experience & Evidence:**
- [ ] Experience section lists 2-4 relevant positions with outcome-focused bullet points
- [ ] Each bullet point includes what was done + for whom + measurable result
- [ ] Target keywords appear 3-5 times across experience entries
- [ ] Education section is filled out and relevant
- [ ] Featured section has 2-3 items supporting the client's goal

**Skills & Social Proof:**
- [ ] At least 5 skills are added, with the top 3 pinned
- [ ] Top 5 skills have at least one endorsement each
- [ ] 2-3 recommendations are present (or have been requested)
- [ ] Projects and certifications are included if applicable

**Settings & Privacy:**
- [ ] Profile update notifications are turned off (during optimization) or back on (if complete)
- [ ] "Open to Work" or availability settings are configured appropriately
- [ ] Contact information is complete and accurate
- [ ] "Make follow primary" is configured appropriately for the client's goal (see Step 13 for rationale)
- [ ] No privacy concerns remain unresolved

**Keyword Distribution Check:**
- [ ] Each of the top 3 target keywords appears approximately 8-10 times total across: Headline (1x each, space permitting), About (2-3x each), Experience (3-5x each), Skills (1x each)
- [ ] **How to verify:** Copy the text from the Headline, About, and Experience sections into a document. Use Ctrl+F (or equivalent) to search for each target keyword and count occurrences. Adjust content if any keyword falls below 7 or exceeds 10.

**Activity Check:**
- [ ] The Activity section shows at least 1 recent post or engagement (a completely empty Activity section signals inactivity)
- [ ] If the client has no posts yet, advise them to share at least 1 piece of relevant content to avoid an empty Activity section

**Cohesion Check:**
- [ ] Read the profile top to bottom as a stranger would. Does it tell a clear, consistent story?
- [ ] Does the profile immediately communicate: who this person is, what they do, who they help, and why it matters?
- [ ] Is there anything contradictory or confusing between sections?

### After the Review

1. Fix any issues identified.
2. Turn profile update notifications back on (if appropriate).
3. Remind the client that a LinkedIn profile is a living document -- it should be updated whenever they have new achievements, skills, roles, or goals.
4. Provide the client with a brief summary of what was done and why, so they understand their own profile.
