# Storytelling Examples

Two anonymized examples showing the 3-angle brainstorming output.

---

## Example 1: The Cross-Domain ML Engineer

**Career data summary:** ML engineer with 8 years of experience who built automated valuation and pricing systems across three different asset classes — consumer goods, financial instruments, and real estate. Academic background in financial economics. Founded an independent AI venture for 3.5 years.

---

### Angle A: "The Valuation Systems Builder"

**Positioning Statement:**
I build AI-powered automated valuation systems that turn messy data into production-ready pricing engines — across any asset class.

**Narrative Arc:**
My career has one consistent thread: I figure out what things are worth, automatically, using data instead of guesswork. I started in financial instruments, moved to consumer marketplaces, and ended up in real estate — building pricing engines in all three. Each domain taught me something the others couldn't, and together they gave me a generalizable framework for valuation that most practitioners never develop.

**Pros:**
- Extremely specific and ownable — very few people can claim cross-domain valuation expertise
- Positions the person as a specialist, not a generalist ML engineer
- "Valuation" is the anchoring word — memorable, searchable, and tied to a clear business problem
- Appeals directly to proptech, fintech, and marketplace companies

**Cons:**
- Narrow — signals that this person does valuation specifically, not "any ML problem"
- May exclude opportunities in ML/AI that aren't pricing-related
- Requires the audience to understand or care about automated valuation as a distinct discipline

**Best For:** Thought Leadership — owning the word "valuation" in the AI/ML space. Also strong for Lead Generation targeting companies with valuation/pricing problems.

---

### Angle B: "The Full-Stack ML Founder"

**Positioning Statement:**
I go from raw unstructured data to deployed ML system — solo. Data pipeline, model development, domain knowledge, and production deployment, all in one.

**Narrative Arc:**
After 8 years of building ML systems inside companies, I spent 3.5 years running my own AI venture — which meant I couldn't hide behind a team. I had to do it all: ingest messy data, engineer features, build and train models, and deploy them to production. That experience taught me that most ML projects fail not because the model is wrong, but because the pipeline between data and deployment is broken. I fix that.

**Pros:**
- Emphasizes end-to-end capability — rare combination that companies struggle to hire for
- The founder angle adds entrepreneurial credibility
- Speaks to a universal pain point (ML projects that don't ship)
- Attracts companies looking for a senior, autonomous engineer

**Cons:**
- Less differentiated — "full-stack ML" is becoming more common as a claim
- Doesn't leverage the deep domain expertise in finance/valuation
- The founder story could raise questions about why they're not still running the venture

**Best For:** Job Seeking — positions the person as a high-value, autonomous hire who can own the entire ML lifecycle.

---

### Angle C: "The Economist Who Codes"

**Positioning Statement:**
I apply financial economics rigor to ML systems — building pricing and valuation models that are both mathematically sound and production-ready.

**Narrative Arc:**
Most ML engineers approach pricing as a prediction problem. I approach it as a financial modeling problem first. My economics training taught me to understand the market dynamics behind the data — time-series behavior, cointegration, supply-demand mechanics. Then my 8 years of ML engineering taught me how to turn that understanding into production systems. The result: models that don't just fit training data, but actually make economic sense.

**Pros:**
- Highlights a rare academic + engineering combination
- Differentiates from pure ML engineers who lack domain depth
- Appeals to finance-heavy audiences (hedge funds, proptech, fintech)
- The "economist who codes" framing is memorable and ownable

**Cons:**
- May signal "academic" to audiences who want pure builders
- Narrows the perceived skill set to finance-adjacent domains
- The economics angle may not resonate with tech-first audiences (startups, SaaS)

**Best For:** Thought Leadership — building authority at the intersection of economics and AI. Strong for speaking, writing, and advisory roles.

## Example 2: The Revenue-Focused Designer

**Career data summary:** UX/product designer with 6 years of experience, moved from agency work to in-house at SaaS companies. Led redesigns that directly increased conversion rates and revenue. Has a background in psychology. Currently freelancing.

---

### Angle A: "The Designer Who Speaks Revenue"

**Positioning Statement:**
I design user experiences that directly increase conversion and revenue — not just look good.

**Narrative Arc:**
I started in design agencies where success was measured in pixels and awards. Then I moved in-house at SaaS companies and discovered that the best design is the one that moves the revenue needle. At my last two roles, I led redesigns that increased conversion by 35% and 28% respectively. I use my psychology background to understand why users bounce — and design to fix it.

**Pros:**
- Directly ties design work to business outcomes — speaks the language of founders and executives
- "Designer who speaks revenue" is a memorable, ownable frame
- Appeals to SaaS founders and growth-stage companies who need design that converts
- Psychology background adds a unique credibility layer

**Cons:**
- May alienate traditional design teams who value craft and aesthetics
- Implies a narrow focus on conversion/CRO — could exclude brand design, product design, research roles
- Revenue attribution for design work can be hard to prove in some contexts

**Best For:** Lead Generation — attracting SaaS founders and growth teams who need design that drives business results.

---

### Angle B: "The Psychology-First Product Designer"

**Positioning Statement:**
I use behavioral psychology to design product experiences that feel intuitive — because they're built around how people actually think.

**Narrative Arc:**
Most designers start with aesthetics. I start with psychology — understanding the cognitive load, decision fatigue, and behavioral triggers that shape how people interact with products. My design process begins with user research and ends with interfaces that feel effortless because they align with mental models, not fight them. Over 6 years, this approach has consistently delivered measurable improvements in engagement and retention.

**Pros:**
- Unique framing that differentiates from other designers
- Psychology angle creates depth and intellectual authority
- Appeals to product-led companies that value research-driven design
- Works well for thought leadership content (writing about behavioral design)

**Cons:**
- More abstract — less immediately compelling to revenue-focused buyers
- "Psychology-first" could signal academic/theoretical rather than practical
- May not resonate with audiences who want to see visual portfolio work first

**Best For:** Thought Leadership — building authority in behavioral/psychology-driven design. Strong for content creation, speaking, and advisory.

---

### Angle C: "The Design Leader for Growing SaaS Teams"

**Positioning Statement:**
I help growing SaaS teams build design systems that scale — from first designer to a functioning design function.

**Narrative Arc:**
I've been the first designer at a startup, the senior designer scaling a team, and the freelancer parachuting into companies that need to level up their design practice. That progression taught me that the biggest design challenge at growing companies isn't the work itself — it's building the systems, processes, and culture that let good design happen consistently. I've done it three times now, and each time the company's design maturity jumped significantly.

**Pros:**
- Targets a specific, common pain point (growing SaaS teams needing design infrastructure)
- The "first designer to design function" arc is relatable and concrete
- Appeals directly to founders and CTOs at Series A/B companies
- Freelance experience becomes a strength, not a gap

**Cons:**
- Positions the person as a design leader/manager rather than hands-on designer
- May not appeal to companies looking for a specialist IC (individual contributor)
- The narrative relies on having been through the scaling journey multiple times — if evidence is thin, it falls apart

**Best For:** Job Seeking — positions the person for design leadership roles at growth-stage SaaS companies. Also works for freelance/consulting engagements.
